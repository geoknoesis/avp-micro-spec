AVP-Micro Payments defines a transport-agnostic layer for programmatic micropayments by autonomous agents: signed quotes, authorizations, executions, and receipts, plus a streaming/session-metering mode. Payer identity, the spending-authority credential, securing mechanisms, and issuer trust are defined by [[DSA]]; this specification composes them with per-transaction economic terms. Value transfer (settlement) is intentionally out of scope as a normative rail.

Given its reliance on Decentralized Identifiers, the Verifiable Credentials Data Model, Data Integrity, and [[DSA]], the intended host is the W3C Credentials Community Group (CCG), or a new CG chartered for agent payments. It is published with W3C ReSpec and does not represent the consensus of the W3C Membership or of any Working or Community Group. Introduction

Autonomous agents increasingly need to purchase APIs, data, and compute at micro scale. Human-centric account flows, opaque bearer tokens, and platform-specific identities poorly match high-frequency, low-value, cross-domain machine transactions. Parallel work on HTTP 402 challenges, streaming payments, and account-to-account APIs improves settlement ergonomics but often assumes a fixed trust boundary.

AVP-Micro Payments composes the portable identity and delegated spending policy layer of [[DSA]] with per-transaction economic terms, orthogonal to any particular settlement technology. A payer agent identifies itself with a decentralized identifier, presents a Spending Authorization Credential (defined by [[DSA]]) that constrains how it may spend, and signs authorization messages that commit to the exact economic terms (amount, currency, settlement target) and bind those terms to a specific service request. A wallet service enforces those constraints before instructing a settlement implementation to move funds. A payee service issues signed quotes and, after settlement, can emit a PaymentReceipt that cryptographically binds delivery to the agreed commercial terms.

This specification depends on [[DSA]], which defines payer identity ([[did-core]]), the Spending Authorization Credential, Merchant Credential, and Payment Capability Credential, the securing mechanisms used to sign every object here, the generic content-digest form and decimal monetary-amount format, category-matching semantics, and issuer trust. It is designed to align with the data models and verification semantics of [[[vc-data-model-2.0]]] and the identifier and resolution model of [[[did-core]]]. It does not define a new DID method.

How it works

AVP-Micro Payments defines how a payer agent buys a service from a payee service in a way that is cryptographically auditable end-to-end. Identity, the spending-authority credential, and trust are defined by [[DSA]]; this specification handles the per-transaction messages.

For a one-off payment: the payee issues a signed PaymentQuote bound to the exact service request; the payer agent constructs a signed PaymentAuthorization that restates the economic terms and embeds a verifiable presentation carrying its Spending Authorization Credential; the wallet verifies the authorization and settles via the chosen rail; the wallet emits a signed PaymentExecution; the payee issues a signed PaymentReceipt binding delivery to the agreed terms. See the one-off payment sequence diagram.

For a streaming or metered session: the payee opens a UsageSession with a budget cap; the payer agent commits with a signed SessionBudgetAuthorization; the payee emits signed UsageAccrual reports as usage accrues; the wallet enforces the budget cap in real time; at close, the payee issues a session PaymentReceipt. The budget cap may be raised mid-session via a UsageSessionExtension followed by a fresh SessionBudgetAuthorization. See the streaming sequence diagram.

Every signed object carries a DataIntegrityProof (ecdsa-jcs-2022), making the full chain — offer, quote, authorization, execution, receipt — independently re-verifiable by an auditor long after the transaction.

Conformance

This specification defines requirements for AVP-Micro data objects and for software that produces or verifies them. All behavior described with normative RFC 2119 language applies to implementations unless a section is explicitly marked non-normative (for example, informative examples or workflow summaries).

An implementation satisfies this specification if it obeys all MUST / MUST NOT requirements applicable to its conformance class. Because this specification builds on [[DSA]], a conforming implementation MUST also satisfy the requirements of [[DSA]] that apply to the credentials, securing mechanisms, and trust decisions it relies on.

Terminology

The keywords MAY, MUST, MUST NOT, OPTIONAL, RECOMMENDED, REQUIRED, SHALL, SHALL NOT, SHOULD, and SHOULD NOT in this document are to be interpreted as described in [[[RFC2119]]] when they appear in uppercase, as suggested by [[[RFC8174]]].

This document uses terms from [[[INFRA]]], including string, map, and list. It also uses, without redefining, the following terms defined by [[DSA]]: Principal, Spending Authorization Credential, Payment Capability Credential, and Merchant Credential.

Payer agent
An autonomous software actor that initiates payment on behalf of a principal (defined by [[DSA]]) and is identified by a DID.
Payee service
A service (merchant, API, or resource provider) that publishes commercial terms, issues Payment quote instances, and delivers goods or services after settlement.
Wallet service
A component or service, controlled by the principal (defined by [[DSA]]), that verifies authorizations and credentials and that instructs a settlement implementation to transfer value.
Verifier / Auditor
An optional party that verifies signatures, credential proofs, and the consistency of the message chain after the fact (for example, for compliance or reconciliation).
Settlement rail
Any technology that moves monetary value between accounts or instruments. AVP-Micro references rails by identifier only; defining or profiling specific rails is out of scope for this specification.
Payment quote
A time-bound, payee-signed commercial offer for a specific request, modeled as a PaymentQuote object.
Economic terms
The tuple (amount, currency, settlementMethod, settlementTarget) that defines how much value moves, in what unit, over which rail, and to which destination. The currency term is defined by [[DSA]] and resolves via the shared context (see ).
Securing mechanism
A cryptographic method (for example a Data Integrity proof or a JWS) that provides integrity and sender authentication for an AVP-Micro data object. Securing mechanisms — including the mandatory-to-implement ecdsa-jcs-2022 cryptosuite, verification-method binding, and DID-state-at-proof-time — are defined by [[DSA]] (see [[DSA]] Securing mechanisms). This document adds only the payment-specific bindings in .
Content digest
A string of the form <alg>:<base64url> binding an object to fixed bytes. The generic content-digest form is defined by [[DSA]]; this document uses it for request and quote binding (see ).
Monetary amount format
The wire format for decimal monetary members (a non-negative decimal string). The format is defined by [[DSA]]; this document restates only the payment-specific strictly-positive rule for transferred or committed amounts (see ).

Namespace and JSON-LD context

The vocabulary IRIs for terms defined in this specification use the HTTPS namespace prefix https://w3id.org/avp-micro/v1#. Implementations MUST treat term definitions in this document as authoritative for interpreting JSON-LD documents that include the AVP-Micro Payments context.

A JSON-LD 1.1 context document is published alongside this specification at context/v1.jsonld and is intended to be served from the canonical URL https://w3id.org/avp-micro/v1. A companion RDFS/OWL ontology at vocab/avp.ttl gives the classes and properties formal RDF semantics; the context and this document remain authoritative for term identity and processing. Deployments MUST use that canonical HTTPS URL for the context value when interoperability across implementations is required. The URL MUST resolve to this context before a publication or conformance profile depends on network dereferencing; until the w3id.org redirect is registered, implementations MUST load an equivalent local context by explicit configuration. The AVP-Micro Payments context defines only payment-message terms and MUST NOT redefine any term from the [[DSA]] context; shared terms (notably currency) and the embedded Spending Authorization Credential resolve from the [[DSA]] context included in the array below.

All AVP-Micro data objects MUST be valid JSON-LD documents when processed with the [[[JSON-LD11]]] algorithms. Every object that carries a proof or a vp member, and every object that is itself a verifiable credential or verifiable presentation, MUST declare its @context as an array containing, in this order, the Verifiable Credentials 2.0 context https://www.w3.org/ns/credentials/v2, the Data Integrity context https://w3id.org/security/data-integrity/v2, the Delegated Spending Authority context https://w3id.org/spending-authority/v1, and the AVP-Micro Payments context https://w3id.org/avp-micro/v1: 

[
  "https://www.w3.org/ns/credentials/v2",
  "https://w3id.org/security/data-integrity/v2",
  "https://w3id.org/spending-authority/v1",
  "https://w3id.org/avp-micro/v1"
]

The Data Integrity context is required because it defines proof terms for objects outside the type-scoped verifiable credential and presentation terms in [[vc-data-model-2.0]], including non-credential payment messages such as PaymentQuote. The [[DSA]] context is included so that the shared currency term and an embedded Spending Authorization Credential (which itself declares the [[DSA]] three-entry context) resolve. The AVP-Micro context defines only payment-message terms. These @protected contexts compose without conflict because this specification reuses existing IRIs rather than redefining them. Because signed objects are canonicalized with [[RFC8785]] (JCS), which preserves array order, the order of the @context entries is part of the signed bytes: producers and verifiers MUST use the array order given above so that proofs are reproducible across implementations.

The AVP-Micro Payments context is declared with "@protected": true and deliberately defines no @vocab fallback. Terms that are not defined by an included context are dropped during JSON-LD expansion rather than coerced to an ad-hoc IRI. Because the mandatory ecdsa-jcs-2022 proof signs the raw JSON document using JCS, such members can still be part of the signed bytes; processors nevertheless MUST NOT make authorization, settlement, or delivery decisions from undefined terms. Extensions therefore MUST ship their own context (see ).

Why array order is significant. The ecdsa-jcs-2022 proof is computed over the JSON Canonicalization Scheme (JCS) serialisation of the object, and JCS preserves JSON array order. Reordering the @context entries produces a different canonical form, a different hash, and therefore an invalid proof — even though the JSON-LD semantics are unchanged.

Because AVP-Micro objects are routinely co-typed or nested with verifiable credentials, the expires and nonce members are mapped to the corresponding Security Vocabulary terms (https://w3id.org/security#expiration and https://w3id.org/security#nonce) rather than to AVP-Micro IRIs. These definitions reuse Security Vocabulary IRIs already used by the proof ecosystem, so the @protected contexts compose without a term-redefinition conflict. The shared currency term is defined once in the [[DSA]] context and reused here. All other members use the avp: namespace.

Until the w3id.org redirects are registered, implementations can load equivalent local context files by explicit configuration or serve them from a controlled origin agreed out of band. The registration of https://w3id.org/avp-micro/v1 and of the [[DSA]] context https://w3id.org/spending-authority/v1 is a prerequisite for the MUST above.

Data model

An AVP-Micro data object is a JSON-LD document whose type includes one of the payment classes defined in this section or in an extension specification. Every party that signs AVP-Micro messages or acts as a named payer, payee, or wallet endpoint MUST be identified with a conforming DID and MUST satisfy the DID-document and verification-method requirements of [[DSA]].

The payer/principal-signed objects here — PaymentAuthorization, PurchaseConfirmation, SessionBudgetAuthorization — are consumable authorization instances: each dsa:exercises the Spending Authorization Credential it presents and dsa:mustMatch the quote it accepts. The payee-signed PaymentQuote, PaymentOffer, and PaymentReceipt are commitments, and a wallet-signed PaymentExecution is a settlement fact; none is an authorization. AVP-Micro deliberately defines only payment-adjacent authorization semantics: the cart/commerce model, credential packaging, bridge provenance, and runtime lifecycle are sibling profiles this specification references rather than absorbs.

Request binding and content digests

AVP-Micro binds a payment to (a) the exact service request being paid for and (b) the exact quote that priced it, using content digest values in the generic content-digest form defined by [[DSA]]. Conforming software MUST support sha-256 and MUST NOT accept a digest whose algorithm it does not recognize.

  • serviceRequestHash is the content digest of the canonical byte serialization of the intended service request (for an HTTP mapping, the request method, target, and body as agreed in the deployment profile). The payee service computes it in the PaymentQuote; the payer agent MUST echo the identical value in the corresponding PaymentAuthorization. The exact canonicalization of the request is deployment-defined but MUST be agreed by both parties and documented in the deployment profile. In the absence of another agreed profile, implementations MUST use the following default request canonicalization: form a JSON object with members method (the HTTP method as an uppercase string), target (the absolute request URL), and body (the request payload as a JSON value, or the base64url-without-padding encoding of the raw body for non-JSON payloads); serialize it with [[RFC8785]] (JCS); and take its sha-256 content digest. This default is the canonicalization used by the published test vectors.
  • quoteDigest is the content digest of the [[RFC8785]] JCS serialization of the PaymentQuote with any proof member removed. The payer agent includes it in the PaymentAuthorization so that the payer's signature commits to the entire quote it observed, not merely its IRI.

Securing mechanisms

Each AVP-Micro object that represents a commitment (see the per-message requirements) MUST be secured with at least one securing mechanism that provides integrity and authenticates the producer. The available securing mechanisms, the mandatory-to-implement DataIntegrityProof with cryptosuite ecdsa-jcs-2022 (P-256) [[VC-DI-ECDSA]], the optional cryptosuites and JOSE-based proofs, the HTTP-message-signing recommendation ([[RFC9421]] / [[RFC9449]]), and the requirement that a verifier reject an object whose only proof uses an unrecognized cryptosuite, are all defined by [[DSA]] (see [[DSA]] Securing mechanisms). The same document defines verification-method binding to a subject DID and the requirement that a proof be verified against the verification method as it existed at proof.created (DID state at proof time). This section adds the payment-specific cross-object bindings.

Verification-method binding (payment authorizations). For any object that names a payer (notably PaymentAuthorization and SessionBudgetAuthorization), the verifying party MUST confirm that the proof.verificationMethod dereferences to a verification method whose controller is the payer DID, and that the same DID equals the referenced quote's or session's payer and the credentialSubject.id of the embedded Spending Authorization Credential (defined by [[DSA]]). A mismatch MUST cause rejection with invalidSignature or credentialRejected. This prevents an attacker who merely possesses a credential from authorizing spending without control of the subject's keys. The underlying verification-method binding and DID-state-at-proof-time requirements are defined by [[DSA]]; this paragraph adds the requirement that the same DID also equal the referenced quote's or session's payer.

Execution signer binding. A PaymentExecution MUST be signed by the wallet service that performed settlement. When the referenced PaymentAuthorization or SessionBudgetAuthorization carries a wallet member naming the authorized wallet DID, a verifier MUST confirm that the execution's proof.verificationMethod dereferences to a verification method controlled by that DID, and MUST reject with invalidSignature on a mismatch. When no wallet member is present, the verifier MUST evaluate the execution's signer against its trust policy (see [[DSA]] Trust establishment) instead, because the authorized wallet identity is then established out of band. Naming wallet in the authorization is therefore RECOMMENDED whenever after-the-fact audit by a third party is expected.

Why naming the authorized wallet matters for audit. Without the wallet member, an auditor re-checking the message chain after the fact cannot determine whether the wallet that signed the execution was the one the payer intended. The wallet member is RECOMMENDED in all deployments and REQUIRED in the auditable profile precisely because its absence makes retrospective audit depend on out-of-band trust.

Why quoteDigest is needed. quoteDigest is a hash of the complete signed quote (proof removed). It commits the payer's signature to the exact price and terms the payer observed — not just to a mutable IRI. Without it, a compromised quote server could swap the quote's terms after the payer signed and before the wallet verified.

Why payee must equal the quote's payee. Without this binding, a payer could reference a legitimate quote from a payee it is not supposed to pay, name a permitted payee DID in the authorization, satisfy the allowedPayees check, and have the wallet settle to the original quote's settlement target. Binding payee in the authorization to the quote's payee closes this policy-bypass.

PurchaseConfirmation

An OPTIONAL object recording a fresh, per-purchase human approval of one exact quoted cart. type MUST include PurchaseConfirmation. Unlike every other message defined here, it is NOT an agent action: it MUST be signed, per , by the principal named in confirmedBy — never by the payer (the agent). Its presence makes a payment human-present; its absence is the default and means the payment proceeds under standing delegation alone (see and [[DSA]]).

The defining rule: signed by the human, not the agent. What makes a PurchaseConfirmation a fresh human approval rather than just another agent action is that its proof MUST be controlled by confirmedBy (the principal) and MUST NOT be controlled by payer (the agent) — a property verifiable from the artifact alone. A verifier MUST reject a confirmation whose proof is signed by any key other than confirmedBy, including one forged by the agent.

Optional and additive. A PurchaseConfirmation is never required by core AVP-Micro flows; standing delegation remains first-class. A verifier that requires fresh human intent — because local policy demands it, or the governing intent signalled it — MUST check that a confirmation is present, is signed by confirmedBy, binds the same quoteDigest, serviceRequestHash, amount, and currency as the PaymentAuthorization it accompanies, and is unexpired. When no such object is present, the payment is autonomous; an importer MUST NOT synthesise one.

The execution's currency MUST equal the currency of the referenced PaymentAuthorization or SessionBudgetAuthorization; a verifier MUST reject an execution whose currency differs.

For a one-off PaymentAuthorization, at most one PaymentExecution with a non-failed status MUST exist; a wallet or verifier that observes a second non-failed execution for the same authorization MUST treat the duplicate as an error rather than a second settlement. (Streaming settlement under a SessionBudgetAuthorization instead admits multiple executions, bounded in aggregate by committedAmount per .) To keep settlement idempotent under retransmission, a wallet SHOULD derive a deterministic settlement key from the authorization identifier and, where the settlement rail supports it, pass that key to the rail so that retrying a pending settlement does not double-spend.

The authorization and sessionBudgetAuthorization references here, and the quote, usageSession, and execution references in a PaymentReceipt, are by IRI and are not accompanied by a content digest — unlike quoteDigest and sessionDigest, which bind upstream objects to their exact bytes. An AVP-Micro verifier re-checking a chain therefore MUST resolve each referenced object to its retained, verbatim signed form (see ) and verify that object's own proof, rather than relying on a referrer's signature to bind the referenced content.

The status term (avp:status) is shared with PaymentReceipt, but its value set differs by object: a PaymentExecution uses {pending, completed, partial, failed} to describe a settlement attempt, whereas a PaymentReceipt uses {fulfilled, partial, failed, reversed} to describe delivery. A consumer MUST interpret status against the enclosing object's type.

Why the unused startingBalance SHOULD be released. startingBalance is a prepayment counted toward the session total. The SHOULD release at close ensures the payer is not charged for unused committed balance. An extension is payee-signed and therefore raises only the payee-set envelope. Monetary amount format

All monetary members (amount, maxAmount, startingBalance, committedAmount, amountAccrued, newMaxAmount, and the credential limits such as maxPerTransaction and dailyLimit that this document references) use the monetary amount format defined by [[DSA]]: a JSON string holding a non-negative decimal number (never a JSON number), matching (0|[1-9][0-9]*)(\.[0-9]+)? (no exponent, no leading + or -, no thousands separators), with fractional digits not exceeding the scale implied by the currency/asset, and with exact decimal arithmetic for sums and limit comparisons. In addition, a member that denotes value actually transferred or committed (amount, amountAccrued, committedAmount), or an upper-bound spending cap (maxAmount, newMaxAmount), MUST be strictly greater than zero, and a verifier MUST reject a message in which such a member is zero or absent where required.

Pricing models

A pricingModel appears in a PaymentOffer (advertised terms) and in a UsageSession (active terms governing accruals). It is either a single rate component or a CompositePricing whose total charge is the sum of its components. All components MUST share one currency.

Rate components

PerCall
A flat amount per invocation.
PerUnit
A linear amount per unit of one dimension measured in unit.
TieredRate
A dimension priced over ordered tiers ({upTo?, amount}; the last omits upTo). tierMode is graduated (each band at its own rate) or volume (the whole quantity at the landed tier's rate).
CommitmentRate
An upfront fee plus a recurring {amount, period} charge. includedQuantity is reserved; its offset semantics are not normative in this version.
Allowance
A freeQuantity subtracted from its dimension before any other component on that dimension is charged.

Dimensions, units, and currency

dimension values are IRIs from the AVP-Micro metering-dimension SKOS scheme (https://w3id.org/avp-micro/dim#); a dimension IRI also serves as a meterType in the session/accrual flow. unit values are QUDT unit IRIs (atomic units, e.g. GigaBYTE vs GibiBYTE) or AVP composite-unit IRIs (https://w3id.org/avp-micro/unit#) defined in terms of a QUDT quantity kind. currency is an ISO 4217 alphabetic code.

Implementations MUST accept the core dimension and unit IRIs registered by this specification. A provider MAY use an extension IRI under .../dim/x/ or .../unit/x/; consumers that do not recognize an extension IRI MUST treat its semantics as unspecified.

Evaluation algorithm

To compute the charge for a usage vector U (a map from dimension IRI to quantity, plus optional calls and periods):

  1. Allowances first. For each Allowance on dimension d, replace U[d] with max(0, U[d] − freeQuantity).
  2. Per component: PerCall → amount × (calls, default 1); PerUnit → amount × U[dimension]; TieredRate/graduated → Σ over tiers of (quantity in band) × amount; TieredRate/volume → U[dimension] × (rate of the landed tier); CommitmentRate → upfront + recurring.amount × (periods, default 1).
  3. Total. Sum the component charges and quantize to 0.00000001 using round-half-up.

All components MUST share the model currency; a mismatch makes the model invalid. The conformance fixtures in test-vectors/pricing-conformance.json pin representative (pricingModel, usage) → amount results.

Streaming and session metering

Streaming mode is used when many small charges accrue over time (token streams, sensor feeds, metered APIs). It avoids issuing a distinct PaymentQuote for every micro-unit by amortizing negotiation: open a UsageSession, authorize the budget once, meter with UsageAccrual, then settle incrementally and/or at close.

Session lifecycle

A UsageSession MUST be treated by conforming implementations as following one of the state sets below; exact state names are informative, but ordering constraints are normative:

  1. Created — Session object exists; payee and payer DIDs, pricingModel, maxAmount, expires, and currency are fixed.
  2. Budget authorized — A SessionBudgetAuthorization was accepted by the wallet service (or equivalent recorded), and the wallet will reject accruals that exceed policy or maxAmount.
  3. Active metering — The payee may issue signed UsageAccrual objects. The wallet MUST NOT apply accruals after expires unless the session is explicitly extended by a UsageSessionExtension and re-authorized per .
  4. Closing — The payer or payee signals end-of-session; no new usage is admitted. Final cumulative charge is computed.
  5. Closed — Final settlement completed (if any balance due remains) and a PaymentReceipt with usageSession and an appropriate status SHOULD be issued.

Accrual evaluation and settlement timing

For each UsageAccrual, the wallet service MUST:

  1. Verify the payee’s proof and that the session is active and unexpired; reject with invalidSignature or sessionExpired respectively.
  2. Verify sequence ordering per ; reject with invalidAccrual on a duplicate or out-of-order value.
  3. Resolve the monetary amount to compare against maxAmount: if accrualKind is cumulative, use amountAccrued directly; if incremental, add to the wallet’s running total for the session.
  4. If meterReading is present, SHOULD verify that the charge is consistent with the session pricingModel and reject anomalous accruals (for example a charge with no corresponding metered usage) with invalidAccrual.
  5. Reject the accrual with accrualExceedsBudget if the resolved total would exceed maxAmount (unless a UsageSessionExtension plus a fresh SessionBudgetAuthorization have raised the cap per ).
  6. If settlementMode is incremental, initiate settlement for the incremental portion (or deployment-defined batch) and record PaymentExecution as needed.

A deployment MUST define how signed UsageAccrual objects are delivered to the wallet: either directly by the payee, through the payer agent, or in a retrievable session log. The wallet MUST acknowledge the highest accepted sequence or cumulative meter state before the payee treats an accrual as admitted. If the wallet observes a gap, missing final accrual, or unacknowledged accrual at close, it MUST hold final settlement for reconciliation rather than silently settling a lower total.

If settlementMode is deferred, the wallet SHOULD still verify each accrual in real time for budget enforcement, then perform one or more settlement operations at session close for the net amount owed.

When both incremental and final settlements occur, the sum of executed amounts MUST equal the final agreed total within the settlement rail’s rounding rules; any release of unused startingBalance SHOULD occur at close.

Integrity for streaming objects

UsageAccrual objects MUST be signed by the payee service so the payer and wallet can prove meter claims. UsageSession objects MUST be signed by the payee when created. SessionBudgetAuthorization MUST be signed by the payer and bound to the payer DID per key binding.

Verifiers MUST reject out-of-order or duplicate sequence values for the same session when sequence is required or present, to limit meter rollback and replay attacks. For incremental accruals, a gap in sequence MUST be treated as a missing charge and the session SHOULD be held for reconciliation rather than silently settled.

Protocol workflows

AVP-Micro is transport-agnostic. Deployments commonly map messages to HTTP (including 402 Payment Required for quotes) or to agent-to-agent channels. The following descriptions are non-normative patterns; normative requirements on message contents and verification are in and .

One-off payment

  1. The payer agent discovers a PaymentOffer (for example via DID document service endpoints).
  2. The agent requests a PaymentQuote bound to a serviceRequestHash of the intended service request; the payee returns a signed quote.
  3. The agent verifies the quote's signature and that its economic terms satisfy the Spending Authorization Credential and local policy.
  4. The agent constructs a signed PaymentAuthorization that restates the economic terms, includes the quoteDigest, and embeds a verifiable presentation.
  5. The wallet service verifies and settles via a settlement rail, emitting a signed PaymentExecution.
  6. The payee service delivers the resource and issues a signed PaymentReceipt.

Streaming and session metering

Normative requirements for sessions, accruals, and settlement timing are in . Typical HTTP-mapped flows follow this pattern:

  1. The payer agent requests session creation; the payee service returns a signed UsageSession (for example 201 Created).
  2. The agent submits a SessionBudgetAuthorization to the wallet service. The wallet verifies the VP and commits up to maxAmount.
  3. During the session, the payee returns signed UsageAccrual updates (for example with HTTP 402 and a JSON body, or alongside streamed data).
  4. Under incremental mode the wallet settles each accrual or batch; under deferred it only enforces the budget until close, then settles the net.
  5. The agent or payee closes the session; the payee finalizes the total and issues a PaymentReceipt with usageSession set.

Wallet verification

One-off PaymentAuthorization

Before initiating settlement for a quoted payment, a wallet service that claims conformance with this specification MUST perform the steps below. The order is normative where one step's result is relied upon by a later step.

Because a PaymentAuthorization carries only the quote's id and quoteDigest — not the quote's bytes — the verifier MUST obtain the complete, payee-signed PaymentQuote before applying the algorithm. A deployment MUST make that quote available to the verifier by either (a) using an id that dereferences to the signed quote, or (b) conveying the signed quote alongside the authorization (for example in the same request envelope). The verifier MUST recompute quoteDigest over the obtained quote (proof removed) and reject with quoteMismatch if it differs, so that the quote the verifier evaluates is provably the one the payer signed over. The same requirement applies to obtaining a UsageSession referenced by a SessionBudgetAuthorization.

  1. Verify the outer integrity of the PaymentAuthorization per and the securing mechanisms of [[DSA]], and confirm the proof is bound to the payer DID per key binding.
  2. Obtain the referenced PaymentQuote as described above, verify the payee's signature on it (reject with invalidSignature on failure), and confirm it is not expired (reject with quoteExpired if past its expires) and its payer equals the authorizing agent’s DID.
  3. Confirm quoteDigest equals the content digest of the resolved quote (proof removed), that the authorization's payee is byte-identical to the quote's payee, and that the authorization's economic terms (amount, currency, settlementMethod, settlementTarget) are each byte-identical to the quote's. Reject with quoteMismatch on any mismatch.
  4. Confirm serviceRequestHash in the authorization is byte-identical to the quote's; reject with quoteMismatch otherwise.
  5. Verify the verifiable presentation and each embedded verifiable credential, including issuer authentication, schema/type, validity window, and revocation/suspension status ([[BITSTRING-STATUS-LIST]]) when present; reject with invalidSignature (proof failure) or credentialRejected (status, schema, or validity failure).
  6. Confirm a Spending Authorization Credential is present whose credentialSubject.id equals the payer DID, and that its issuer is acceptable under the wallet's trust policy (see [[DSA]] Trust establishment); reject with credentialRejected otherwise.
  7. Enforce numeric and categorical constraints against the credential and local policy, applying the more restrictive of the two: amount ≤ maxPerTransaction; day-aggregated spend + amount ≤ dailyLimit (); currency matches; payee in allowedPayees and/or category match per [[DSA]] category matching. Reject with policyViolation if any limit or category check fails. The maxPerTransaction, dailyLimit, allowedPayees, and allowedServiceTypes claim semantics are defined by [[DSA]].
  8. Reject reused nonce values within the verifier’s retention window, and reject stale timestamp/expires per the clock-skew budget (), with nonceReuse in either case.
  9. If Payment Capability Credential instances are required by policy, validate them before sending funds: verify the credential proof, confirm the issuer is an accepted wallet provider or financial institution under local trust policy, confirm credentialSubject.id equals the payer DID, confirm any currency, asset, assetScale, and account claims match the authorization's settlement target and economic terms, and reject expired credentials.

SessionBudgetAuthorization

Before accepting metering charges for a UsageSession, the wallet MUST:

  1. Verify the payer’s signature on SessionBudgetAuthorization, bound to the payer DID per key binding (reject with invalidSignature), and check nonce, timestamp, and expires (reject with nonceReuse).
  2. Resolve the UsageSession, verify the payee signature, confirm it is unexpired (reject with sessionExpired), that sessionDigest matches and the payer/payee match (reject with sessionInvalid), that the payee DID is acceptable, and that committedAmount ≤ session maxAmount with matching currency (reject with policyViolation).
  3. Verify the VP and Spending Authorization Credential such that session maxAmount and payee fall within credential limits. If Payment Capability Credential instances are required by policy, validate them as in the one-off flow and confirm their account/currency claims are compatible with the session settlement target.
  4. Record the authorization and associate it with usageSession for subsequent UsageAccrual processing per .

Streaming accruals

During UsageSession processing, the wallet MUST apply on every UsageAccrual and MUST ensure that evaluated charges never exceed maxAmount unless a UsageSessionExtension and a fresh SessionBudgetAuthorization raise the cap per .

Daily-limit aggregation

The dailyLimit claim, the meaning of the day boundary (the calendar day in the credential's limitTimezone, or UTC if that member is absent), and the limitTimezone claim are defined by [[DSA]]. This section specifies the cross-transaction enforcement a wallet service performs. A wallet that enforces dailyLimit MUST aggregate completed and in-flight authorizations for the subject across the accounts it controls and MUST serialize the check-and-reserve step (for example with a per-subject lock or atomic counter) so that concurrent authorizations cannot jointly exceed the cap. If a deployment cannot guarantee a single aggregation point (for example multiple independent wallets share one credential), it MUST document that dailyLimit enforcement is best-effort and SHOULD compensate with a lower maxPerTransaction or reconciliation.

Integrity, authentication, and replay protection

Object-level securing mechanisms are defined in and by [[DSA]]. When AVP-Micro objects are carried over HTTP, implementations SHOULD sign the HTTP requests and responses using [[RFC9421]] or use [[RFC9449]] proof-of-possession where OAuth infrastructure is present. Other transports MUST provide equivalent integrity and sender authentication.

PaymentAuthorization and SessionBudgetAuthorization objects MUST include nonce, timestamp, and expires to limit replay. Verifiers MUST reject messages outside a deployment-defined clock-skew budget; absent a profile, the RECOMMENDED default budget is 300 seconds. Verifiers MUST retain seen nonce values at least until expires plus the skew budget so that the replay window is fully covered. nonce uniqueness is scoped to the (payer, verifier) pair.

Why nonce uniqueness is scoped to the payer–verifier pair. A payer using two independent wallet services for different merchants can reuse the same nonce string without enabling cross-wallet replay, because the verifier component of the pair differs. Implementations need only retain nonces within the window [issue, expires + skew budget] for each (payer, verifier) pair.

Disputes, refunds, and reversals

Payment can succeed while delivery fails (serviceError), partially completes (partial), or is contested. AVP-Micro keeps a signed, append-only chain so such cases are adjudicable after the fact.

  • A payee service that received settlement but cannot deliver SHOULD issue a signed PaymentReceipt with status set to failed or partial, or initiate a rail-level reversal, and SHOULD reference the original execution.
  • The reversal of value itself happens on the settlement rail and is out of scope; AVP-Micro provides the evidence (matched authorization, execution, and receipt) needed to substantiate a dispute.
  • Extension specifications MAY define Refund or Reversal objects that reference a prior execution and carry their own proof; such objects MUST be defined in a dedicated context per .

Extensions and interoperability

New credential types, pricing models, settlement method strings, and transports can be defined without changing the core message types. Because the AVP-Micro Payments context defines no @vocab fallback, extension specifications MUST register their terms in a dedicated JSON-LD context, MUST NOT redefine core terms, and instances MUST include the extension context alongside the core context.

Anticipated extensions include Refund / Reversal objects () and a DelegatedAuthorizationCredential supporting multi-step delegation chains (for example organization → department → agent), in which each credential authorizes issuance of a more constrained credential further down the chain and a verifier walks the chain to a trusted root. Such delegation is out of scope for this version; the single-issuer Spending Authorization Credential model defined by [[DSA]] is normative here.

AVP-Micro is compatible with [[OPEN-PAYMENTS]] account semantics, [[GNAP]] grant negotiation where used by a wallet deployment, and HTTP payment challenge flows that carry authorization objects in headers or bodies, provided the data model in is preserved.

Audit, logging, and reconciliation

Parties SHOULD retain append-only records of offers, quotes, authorizations, executions, receipts, UsageSession objects, UsageAccrual series, and SessionBudgetAuthorization messages sufficient to reconstruct the signed message chain for reconciliation and dispute resolution. Because each object carries its own proof, storing the verbatim signed JSON is sufficient as audit evidence. Receipts MAY be represented as verifiable credentials for third-party verification. Logs SHOULD NOT retain private keys or full credential payloads beyond what reconciliation requires.

Security considerations

AVP-Micro moves high-value decisions (spending) across trust boundaries. Implementations MUST protect private signing keys, SHOULD isolate agent keys in hardware or a separate process, and MUST evaluate issuer trust (see [[DSA]] Trust establishment) before honoring credentials.

Settlement rails have their own threat models (finality, chargebacks, privacy of payment pointers). This specification does not alter those properties; it adds an auditable authorization layer above them. Deployments SHOULD constrain quote and authorization lifetimes to the minimum operationally acceptable window.

Privacy considerations

Agents SHOULD disclose the minimum verifiable claims necessary for a transaction. Selective disclosure is not a property of the base [[vc-data-model-2.0]]; deployments that need it SHOULD use a mechanism that provides it, such as SD-JWT VC [[SD-JWT-VC]] or a Data Integrity cryptosuite with selective disclosure. Pairwise identifiers are compatible with these mechanisms.

Correlation can arise from DID resolution, repeated payer DIDs, issuer identifiers, and settlement metadata. High-privacy deployments SHOULD analyze timing and identifier rotation, and SHOULD NOT place principal or end-user PII in a Spending Authorization Credential, which travels to wallets and potentially payees.

Long-lived UsageSession identifiers and repeated UsageAccrual traffic can fingerprint agents. Deployments MAY use shorter sessions, rotated session IDs, or minimal meter fields where policy allows.

Internationalization considerations

The currency member SHOULD carry an ISO 4217 alphabetic code for fiat currencies; currency and asset formatting are defined by [[DSA]]. A deployment MAY use a non-ISO asset code (for example a token symbol); when it does, both parties MUST agree on the code's meaning and scale out of band, and an assetScale SHOULD be supplied. Decimal amounts use a . as the decimal separator regardless of locale (see ); locale-specific formatting is a presentation concern and MUST NOT appear in the wire format. Human-readable strings such as merchantName MAY use VC 2.0 language-tagged values.

Accessibility considerations

AVP-Micro defines machine-to-machine data objects and a verification algorithm; it has no user interface and therefore introduces no direct accessibility requirements. User agents that surface AVP-Micro information to people — for example a wallet prompting for approval under requiresApprovalAbove, or a tool that renders a PaymentReceipt — are expected to follow general Web accessibility guidance ([[WCAG22]]) for that presentation, and any human-readable strings carried in messages should use VC 2.0 language-tagged values () so assistive technologies can present them correctly.

Conformance classes

This specification defines the following conformance classes:

AVP-Micro payer agent
Software that produces conforming PaymentAuthorization and/or SessionBudgetAuthorization messages and associated verifiable presentations. It MUST restate and sign the economic terms and MUST honor requiresApprovalAbove.
AVP-Micro wallet
Software that implements and produces signed PaymentExecution records, including for streaming mode when supported. It MUST perform verification-method binding (key binding and [[DSA]] Securing mechanisms) and trust evaluation (see [[DSA]] Trust establishment).
AVP-Micro payee
Software that issues PaymentOffer advertisements and signed PaymentQuote and PaymentReceipt messages, and MAY issue signed UsageSession and UsageAccrual for streaming. Quotes, receipts, sessions, and accruals it emits MUST be secured per .
AVP-Micro verifier
Software (for example an auditor) that, given a retained message chain, re-verifies all proofs, the verification-method bindings, the digest and economic-term equalities, and the budget arithmetic, without initiating settlement. It MUST resolve DID state as of proof.created (see [[DSA]] Securing mechanisms) and MUST apply execution-signer binding (execution binding).

Two interoperability profiles let implementers adopt a small surface first and add auditability later:

core profile
The minimal interoperable target: the one-off flow (PaymentQuote → PaymentAuthorization → PaymentExecution → PaymentReceipt) with the mandatory-to-implement securing mechanism. A core implementation MUST produce and verify each object's ecdsa-jcs-2022 proof and enforce the one-off wallet-verification checks, but MAY omit streaming () and is not required to populate the optional wallet member. As noted in [[DSA]] (securing mechanisms), a core implementation needs only a JCS canonicalizer, a P-256 ECDSA implementation, and the JSON Schema — not a JSON-LD/RDF toolchain.
auditable profile
A superset claimed when a message chain is intended to be re-verified by an AVP-Micro verifier the deployment does not control. In the auditable profile, every PaymentAuthorization and SessionBudgetAuthorization MUST populate the wallet member (execution binding), so that the authorized wallet identity is recoverable from the signed objects alone. Outside this profile the wallet member remains RECOMMENDED.

The published test vectors together with the shared verification and validation harness constitute a reference interop suite for both profiles: a conforming implementation SHOULD verify every published vector and reproduce the signed one-off and streaming chains.

Security and Privacy Self-Review

This appendix answers the questions of the W3C TAG Self-Review Questionnaire: Security and Privacy [[SECURITY-PRIVACY-QUESTIONNAIRE]] for the payments subset; the identity, credential, and trust answers are in [[DSA]]. AVP-Micro defines data objects and a verification algorithm rather than a browser feature, so several questions are answered as not applicable.

What information does this feature expose, and for what purposes?
Payer/payee/issuer DIDs, the economic terms of a transaction, the constraints in a Spending Authorization Credential, and digests of the service request and output. The purpose is to let a wallet service authorize spending and let a verifier audit it. Objects expose only what is needed to evaluate a single transaction; see the next answer.
Do features expose the minimum information necessary?
Agents are directed to disclose the minimum verifiable claims (). The base [[vc-data-model-2.0]] lacks selective disclosure, so deployments needing it are pointed to [[SD-JWT-VC]] or a selective-disclosure cryptosuite. PII SHOULD NOT be placed in credentials that travel to wallets and payees.
Do features handle personally identifiable or financial information?
Yes — value transfer. Amounts are committed to by signatures; limits and category constraints bound exposure; settlement metadata is rail-specific and out of scope. Correlation risks (repeated DIDs, issuer identifiers, session IDs, timing) are addressed in .
Does this feature introduce new state that persists across sessions?
Verifiers retain seen nonce values for the replay window () and parties retain a signed message chain for audit (). The specification directs that private keys and full credential payloads not be retained beyond reconciliation needs.
How does the feature deal with information from origins a user is not interacting with / third parties?
Trust is never inherited from the message sender: a wallet MUST evaluate credential issuers against its own configured trust roots and MUST NOT treat a payee's acceptedCredentialIssuers as a trust root (see [[DSA]] Trust establishment).
What are the feature's safe defaults, and what happens on error?
Undefined JSON-LD terms are not given protocol semantics (@protected, no @vocab), processors MUST NOT make authorization, settlement, or delivery decisions from them, unrecognized cryptosuites are rejected, absent credential constraints are treated as not permitted by the credential rather than permissive, and every denial path has an explicit error code () and an audit receipt.
What are the risks of abuse, and the mitigations?
The threat/mitigation table in enumerates term and request tampering, over-spending, inflated streaming charges, and term injection, together with the mechanisms that address each; credential theft/replay and untrusted issuers are addressed by [[DSA]].
Does the feature expose sensors, the underlying platform, native UI, or allow access to other devices?
Not applicable; AVP-Micro is a transport-agnostic data model.
How does the feature behave in private-browsing/incognito modes?
Not applicable as a browser feature. Agents that need unlinkability are directed to pairwise/rotated identifiers and short sessions ().
