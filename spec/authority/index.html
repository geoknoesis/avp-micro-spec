Delegated Spending Authority (DSA) defines how a principal delegates bounded spending authority to an autonomous payer agent using decentralized identifiers and verifiable credentials, and how a relying party establishes trust in the issuers of those credentials. It defines the identity, securing, credential, and trust vocabulary that the AVP-Micro payments layer (and other delegated-spend systems) build upon. Settlement and payment-message flow are out of scope. DSA is domain-neutral: it bounds how much, to whom, and under what policy an agent may spend, and makes no assumption about the domain in which spending occurs or what is purchased.

Introduction

Autonomous agents increasingly need to pay for things at micro scale — APIs, data, compute, content, access, or goods, depending on the application. Human-centric account flows, opaque bearer tokens, and platform-specific identities poorly match high-frequency, low-value, cross-domain machine transactions. A portable, transport-agnostic layer for identity and delegated spending policy is needed that is orthogonal both to any particular settlement technology and to the domain in which spending occurs.

Delegated Spending Authority adds that layer. A payer agent identifies itself with a decentralized identifier, and a principal that controls funds issues verifiable credentials that constrain how the agent may spend — currency, per-transaction and daily ceilings, allowed payees, and allowed payee categories. A relying party verifies those credentials, binds them to the agent's keys, and evaluates the issuer against an explicit trust policy before honoring the delegated authority. This standing, transaction-independent authority is separable from, and reusable by, any payment or settlement flow built on top of it.

This specification is designed to align with the data models and verification semantics of [[[vc-data-model-2.0]]] and the identifier and resolution model of [[[did-core]]]. It does not define a new DID method.

How it works

Delegated Spending Authority defines how an organisation (the principal) grants a bounded right to spend to an autonomous payer agent. The grant is encoded in a Spending Authorization Credential — a Verifiable Credential that names the agent DID as its subject and carries constraints: maximum amount per transaction, a daily cap, a list of approved payees, and acceptable payee categories.

A relying party (a wallet service or verifier) that receives a message signed by the agent first verifies the Spending Authorization Credential and checks that its issuer is listed in the relying party's local TrustedIssuer configuration. Optionally, an IssuerScope further restricts what that issuer is trusted to authorise — the more restrictive of the credential and the scope applies.

Trust in payee services is established via a Merchant Credential, issued by a third-party trust anchor such as an industry registry. A relying party can match the payee's attested payee categories against the agent's allowedCategories to enforce categorical spending policies.

Signed commitments are secured with cryptographic proofs. The baseline mandatory-to-implement mechanism is the W3C Data Integrity ecdsa-jcs-2022 cryptosuite with P-256 keys exposed through did:key for interoperability testing; deployments can use additional DID methods and cryptosuites when all relying parties support them. Conformance

This specification defines requirements for DSA data objects and for software that produces or verifies them. All behavior described with normative RFC 2119 language applies to implementations unless a section is explicitly marked non-normative (for example, informative examples or workflow summaries).

An implementation satisfies this specification if it obeys all MUST / MUST NOT requirements applicable to its conformance class.

Terminology

The keywords MAY, MUST, MUST NOT, OPTIONAL, RECOMMENDED, REQUIRED, SHALL, SHALL NOT, SHOULD, and SHOULD NOT in this document are to be interpreted as described in [[[RFC2119]]] when they appear in uppercase, as suggested by [[[RFC8174]]].

This document uses terms from [[[INFRA]]], including string, map, and list.

Principal
An entity (person or organization) that controls funds and that MAY issue Spending Authorization Credential instances to one or more payer agents. A principal that issues credentials MUST be identified by a conforming DID ([[did-core]], ) and is the issuer of the Spending Authorization Credential it grants; a principal MAY delegate credential issuance to a wallet service or other provider acting on its behalf, in which case the issuer DID is that of the delegate and the relying party's trust policy applies to the delegate.
Payer agent
An autonomous software actor that initiates payment on behalf of a principal and is identified by a DID.
Economic terms
The tuple (amount, currency, settlementMethod, settlementTarget) that defines how much value moves, in what unit, over which rail, and to which destination.
Securing mechanism
A cryptographic method (for example a Data Integrity proof or a JWS) that provides integrity and sender authentication for a DSA data object, as profiled in .

Namespace and JSON-LD context

The vocabulary IRIs for terms defined in this specification use the HTTPS namespace prefix https://w3id.org/spending-authority/v1# (prefix dsa:). Implementations MUST treat term definitions in this document as authoritative for interpreting JSON-LD documents that include the Delegated Spending Authority context.

A JSON-LD 1.1 context document is published alongside this specification at context/v1.jsonld and is intended to be served from the canonical URL https://w3id.org/spending-authority/v1. A companion RDFS/OWL ontology at vocab/dsa.ttl gives the classes and properties formal RDF semantics; the context and this document remain authoritative for term identity and processing. Deployments MUST use that canonical HTTPS URL for the context value when interoperability across implementations is required. The URL MUST resolve to this context before a publication or conformance profile depends on network dereferencing; until the w3id.org redirect is registered, implementations MUST load an equivalent local context by explicit configuration.

All DSA data objects MUST be valid JSON-LD documents when processed with the [[[JSON-LD11]]] algorithms. Every object that carries a proof or a vp member, and every object that is itself a verifiable credential or verifiable presentation, MUST declare its @context as an array containing, in this order, the Verifiable Credentials 2.0 context https://www.w3.org/ns/credentials/v2, the Data Integrity context https://w3id.org/security/data-integrity/v2, and the Delegated Spending Authority context https://w3id.org/spending-authority/v1. The Data Integrity context is required because it defines proof terms for objects outside the type-scoped verifiable credential and presentation terms in [[vc-data-model-2.0]]. The DSA context maps expires and nonce to Security Vocabulary IRIs rather than redefining them, so the @protected contexts compose without conflict. Because signed objects are canonicalized with [[RFC8785]] (JCS), which preserves array order, the order of the @context entries is part of the signed bytes: producers and verifiers MUST use the array order given above so that proofs are reproducible across implementations.

Why this matters for security. The Delegated Spending Authority context is declared with "@protected": true and deliberately defines no @vocab fallback. Terms that are not defined by an included context are dropped during JSON-LD expansion rather than coerced to an ad-hoc IRI. Because the mandatory ecdsa-jcs-2022 proof signs the raw JSON document using JCS, such members can still be part of the signed bytes; processors nevertheless MUST NOT make authorization decisions from undefined terms. Extensions therefore MUST ship their own context.

Because DSA objects are routinely co-typed or nested with verifiable credentials, the expires and nonce members are mapped to the corresponding Security Vocabulary terms (https://w3id.org/security#expiration and https://w3id.org/security#nonce) rather than to DSA IRIs. These definitions reuse Security Vocabulary IRIs already used by the proof ecosystem, so the @protected contexts compose without a term-redefinition conflict. All other members use the dsa: namespace.

Until the w3id.org redirects are registered, implementations can load equivalent local context files by explicit configuration or serve them from a controlled origin agreed out of band. The registration of https://w3id.org/spending-authority/v1 and https://w3id.org/avp-micro/cat is a prerequisite for the MUST above.

Agent service categories (SKOS)

Data model

A DSA data object is a JSON-LD document whose type includes one of the classes defined in this section or in an extension specification.

Authorization model: capacity vs instance

An authorization is the semantic concept of granted permission. It comes in exactly two kinds, distinguished by whether the artifact may legitimately be presented more than once:

  • An authorization capacity (dsa:AuthorizationCapacity) is standing authority — who may authorize and within what bounds. It is re-presentable, revocable, and delegable; revocation and delegation are properties of capacity. A Spending Authorization Credential is a capacity.
  • An authorization instance (dsa:AuthorizationInstance) is a single consumable act — what was authorized, here and now. It is nonce-bound, single-use, merchant-bound, and liability-bearing; replay protection, liability, and audit attach to instances. In [[AVP-MICRO-PAYMENTS]] a PaymentAuthorization, PurchaseConfirmation, and SessionBudgetAuthorization are instances.

A payee-signed quote, offer, or receipt is a commitment, and a wallet-signed execution is a settlement fact; neither is an authorization, and neither is a dsa:Authorization.

An instance dsa:exercises the capacity it acts under and MUST dsa:conformsTo its bounds; a verifier MUST reject an instance whose terms exceed the capacity it exercises. An instance also dsa:mustMatch the commitment it references, realized by digest binding (quoteDigest, requestHash). These are semantic authorization relations; they are distinct from provenance relations (prov:wasDerivedFrom), which describe artifact transformation and belong to the interop/securing layer — the two families MUST NOT be conflated. A "mandate" is the role an authorization artifact plays when used as binding evidence; it is carried by a credential, and is not itself one.

DID documents and service endpoints

Every party that signs DSA messages or acts as a named payer, payee, or wallet endpoint MUST be identified with a conforming DID as defined in [[[did-core]]]. For interoperability testing, conforming software MUST support the did:key method [[DID-KEY]] with P-256 (secp256r1) keys as the mandatory-to-implement interoperability method and SHOULD support did:web. A deployment MAY require additional DID methods by profile.

A DID document used with DSA MUST contain at least one verification method suitable for proof verification, which MUST be of the Multikey verification method type [[VC-DATA-INTEGRITY]] (a P-256 key encoded as publicKeyMultibase, multicodec p256-pub). A DID document MAY advertise HTTP or other service endpoints for quotes, wallet APIs, credential status, or receipts using service entries. Specific serviceEndpoint types and discovery rules are deployment-specific; this specification defines the data carried in messages, not URL templates for every API.

Securing mechanisms

Each DSA object that represents a commitment (see the per-message requirements) MUST be secured with at least one securing mechanism that provides integrity and authenticates the producer. Conforming software:

  • MUST support Data Integrity proofs [[VC-DATA-INTEGRITY]] of type DataIntegrityProof with cryptosuite ecdsa-jcs-2022 [[VC-DI-ECDSA]] over the P-256 (secp256r1) curve as the mandatory-to-implement object proof;
  • MAY additionally support ecdsa-rdfc-2019 [[VC-DI-ECDSA]]; the EdDSA cryptosuites eddsa-jcs-2022 / eddsa-rdfc-2022 [[VC-DI-EDDSA]] for pure-W3C deployments where every relying party supports them; or JOSE-based proofs (JsonWebSignature2020 / detached JWS [[RFC7515]]).

The ecdsa-jcs-2022 cryptosuite over P-256 is mandated as the baseline for two reasons. First, like the EdDSA JCS suite it relies on the JSON Canonicalization Scheme [[RFC8785]] rather than RDF dataset canonicalization, which keeps signing and verification straightforward for the flat objects defined here. Second, P-256 is the curve that hardware key custody and the surrounding agent-payment ecosystem already speak: secure enclaves, TPMs, and FIDO/WebAuthn authenticators natively hold P-256 keys (Ed25519 is widely unavailable in that hardware), and the JOSE ES256 credentials of Mastercard/Google Verifiable Intent and Google AP2 (see the interop profile) use the same curve — so a single key can both produce the Data Integrity proof here and a co-issued ES256 form there.

To retain the misuse resistance that EdDSA provides for free, a conforming producer MUST derive ECDSA nonces deterministically per [[RFC6979]] and emit signatures in canonical low-s form (IEEE P1363 raw R/S); a verifier MUST reject the malleable high-s variant. A conforming verifier MUST also reject an object whose only proof uses a cryptosuite it does not recognize.

A JSON-LD or RDF toolchain is not required to produce or verify the mandatory proof. Because ecdsa-jcs-2022 canonicalizes with [[RFC8785]] (JCS) over the JSON document itself — not over an RDF dataset — a conforming producer or verifier needs only a JCS canonicalizer and a P-256 ECDSA implementation, both of which are widely available. The accompanying JSON Schema validates the same plain JSON. A JSON-LD processor is needed only to consume these objects as RDF — for semantic integration, ontology reasoning, or SHACL validation — and the requirement that every object be valid JSON-LD () exists so that such consumers interoperate, not to oblige every implementer to run a processor. Tamper resistance does not depend on JSON-LD processing: any added, removed, or altered member changes the JCS bytes and therefore breaks the signature, so a plain-JSON verifier that recomputes JCS over the received object is as safe as an RDF-aware one.

Verification-method binding. For any object that names a payer and embeds a Spending Authorization Credential, the verifying party MUST confirm that the proof.verificationMethod dereferences to a verification method whose controller is the payer DID, and that the same DID equals the credentialSubject.id of the embedded Spending Authorization Credential. A mismatch MUST cause rejection with invalidSignature or credentialRejected. This prevents an attacker who merely possesses a credential from authorizing spending without control of the subject's keys.

Why key binding closes the "credential theft" attack. An entity that obtains a Spending Authorization Credential — say by intercepting it in transit — cannot use it to authorise spending unless it also holds the corresponding private key. The proof.verificationMethod check is what ties the credential's authority to the specific key. Mere possession of the credential is not sufficient.

DID state at proof time. A proof MUST be verified against the verification method as it existed in the signer's DID document at the time the proof was created (the proof.created instant). For DID methods whose documents can change (for example did:web or ledger-based methods), a verifier performing after-the-fact audit MUST resolve the DID document version current at proof.created — using the method's versioning facilities or a retained, dated copy of the resolved document — rather than the latest state, so that key rotation or other document changes occurring after signing neither invalidate a historically valid proof nor let a rotated-in key forge one. The mandatory-to-implement did:key method is immutable and is therefore unaffected.

Content digests

A content digest is a string of the form <alg>:<base64url>, where <alg> is a hash algorithm identifier and <base64url> is the base64url encoding (without padding, per [[RFC7515]] §2) of the digest bytes. Conforming software MUST support sha-256 and MUST NOT accept a digest whose algorithm it does not recognize. Example: sha-256:qa1n6G7l5b....

This generic content-digest form is used by credential and trust objects to bind to opaque byte sequences (for example a request or output digest). Specifications that build on Delegated Spending Authority reuse this form to define their own digest members.

Credential types

Delegated Spending Authority defines three credential profiles as layered types on the [[vc-data-model-2.0]]. Each profile MUST be serialized as a verifiable credential using the VC 2.0 context and MUST include a proof satisfying a securing mechanism from . Credentials SHOULD carry a credentialStatus entry using [[BITSTRING-STATUS-LIST]] so that verifiers can check revocation and suspension; verifiers MUST honor status when present.

Spending Authorization Credential

Issued by a principal to a payer agent. The type array MUST include VerifiableCredential and SpendingAuthorizationCredential.

The credentialSubject MUST be a map whose id is the payer agent’s DID. The subject MAY include the following claims; a verifier MUST treat an absent constraint as not granting authority for that constraint dimension and MUST NOT substitute a more permissive default. Where a deployment policy imposes an additional limit, the more restrictive of credential and policy applies.

A verifiable credential issued by a principal to a payer agent, encoding the principal's spending-authority constraints as claims on the agent DID.

credentialSubject claims
Member Req. Type Description
id REQUIRED DID The payer agent's DID (the credential subject).
currency OPTIONAL string ISO 4217 alphabetic currency code or deployment-defined asset code (see ).
maxPerTransaction OPTIONAL decimal string Maximum amount the agent may spend in a single payment.
dailyLimit OPTIONAL decimal string Aggregate cap per calendar day. Day boundary is determined by limitTimezone.
limitTimezone OPTIONAL string IANA time-zone name defining the day boundary for dailyLimit; absent means UTC.
allowedPayees OPTIONAL list of DIDs Payee DIDs the agent is permitted to pay. Values MUST be DIDs; pattern matching is out of scope.
allowedCategories OPTIONAL list of IRIs Service-category IRIs (typically cat: concepts) the agent may pay; matching per .
requiresApprovalAbove OPTIONAL decimal string Threshold above which the agent MUST obtain out-of-band human approval. This is an agent-side obligation outside cryptographic enforcement.

The credential's overall validity window uses the [[vc-data-model-2.0]] top-level validFrom / validUntil members ([[RFC3339]] timestamps); these are inherited from the VC 2.0 context and are not redefined by Delegated Spending Authority nor carried as subject claims. Verifiers MUST reject a credential used outside that window. An absent validFrom imposes no lower bound and an absent validUntil no upper bound; a credential with neither is unbounded in time and is invalidated only by revocation or suspension (credentialStatus, ). Issuers SHOULD set validUntil so that authority is time-limited even if status cannot be checked.

Payment Capability Credential

Issued by a wallet provider or financial institution. The type array MUST include VerifiableCredential and PaymentCapabilityCredential.

A verifiable credential attesting a payer agent's payment capability — account address, currency, and optional asset scale — issued by the wallet provider or financial institution.

A Payment Capability Credential binds a payer agent DID to a wallet account or payment capability. When a relying party requires this credential, it MUST verify the issuer under local trust policy, verify the credential proof, confirm credentialSubject.id is the payer agent DID, reject expired credentials, and apply any account, currency, asset, or assetScale claims as constraints on the settlement target and economic terms.

credentialSubject claims
Member Req. Type Description
id REQUIRED DID The payer agent's DID.
account SHOULD string Payment pointer, account URL, or on-ledger address.
currency SHOULD string ISO 4217 currency code.
asset OPTIONAL string Non-ISO asset code (for example a token symbol).
assetScale OPTIONAL integer ≥ 0 Number of decimal places for the asset.
expires OPTIONAL RFC 3339 Capability expiry; relying parties MUST reject a credential used after this instant.

Merchant Credential

Optional attestation about a payee service, issued by a trust anchor (see ). The type array MUST include VerifiableCredential and MerchantCredential. The subject MUST have an id equal to the payee DID.

A verifiable credential issued by a trust anchor (such as an industry registry) attesting a payee service's identity and the service categories it provides.

credentialSubject claims
Member Req. Type Description
id REQUIRED DID The payee service's DID.
merchantName / companyName SHOULD string Human-readable name of the payee service or organisation. May use VC 2.0 language-tagged values.
categories SHOULD list of IRIs Service-category IRIs from the agreed concept scheme. These are third-party attested and used for policy-enforcement matching (see ).

The term payee service is used for the role; the credential type and the merchantName claim retain the word merchant only because it is the established label for a business-identity attestation in existing commerce and trust-registry ecosystems. The two terms denote the same role here.

Category matching

Categories can appear in two places with different trust levels: a Merchant Credential carries categories attested by a third-party trust anchor, whereas a payment offer carries categories self-asserted by the payee. For policy enforcement, a wallet MUST use only categories from a Merchant Credential whose issuer is acceptable under its trust policy; it MUST NOT treat self-asserted payment-offer categories as satisfying an allowedCategories constraint, since a payee could assert any category. Self-asserted categories MAY be used only for discovery, routing, or presentation.

Where a payee service asserts categories in a Merchant Credential and the agent's Spending Authorization Credential constrains allowedCategories, a wallet that enforces categorical constraints MUST require that at least one attested category IRI is authorized by allowedCategories within the agreed concept scheme — that is, the asserted category is identical to a member of allowedCategories, or some member of allowedCategories is a skos:broaderTransitive ancestor of the asserted category. Equivalently, the asserted category MUST be the same as, or narrower than, an allowed type; a payee asserting only a category broader than every allowed type does not match. Because the published vocabulary asserts only direct skos:broader relations, implementations MUST compute the transitive closure of skos:broader (per [[SKOS]] semantics) when evaluating this condition. Comparison is over absolute IRIs. If the agent constrains allowedCategories but the payee asserts no verifiable category, the wallet MUST reject with policyViolation.

Why the match direction matters. The rule is "the asserted category must be the same as, or narrower than, an allowed type" — not the reverse. A payee self-asserting a broader category (e.g. cat:ComputeAndInference) does not satisfy an agent restricted to a narrower one (e.g. cat:ChatCompletionApi). This prevents a payee from claiming a permissive parent category to bypass a specific restriction.

Daily-limit claim and day boundary

When dailyLimit is present in a Spending Authorization Credential, the day boundary is the calendar day in the credential's limitTimezone, or UTC if that member is absent. The claim caps aggregate spend by the subject across that calendar day. The cross-transaction aggregation and serialization requirements that a wallet applies when enforcing this claim are defined by the payments processing layer that consumes the credential; this specification defines only the claim and the day boundary it is measured against.

Why the timezone is in the credential. "End of day" is ambiguous. Without limitTimezone a daily limit resets at UTC midnight, which may not match the principal's operational timezone. Encoding the intended timezone in the credential itself lets the wallet enforce the correct day boundary regardless of where it is deployed.

Monetary amount format

All monetary members (amount, maxPerTransaction, dailyLimit, maxDailyTotal) MUST be JSON strings holding a decimal number, never JSON numbers, to avoid binary floating-point rounding. The string MUST match (0|[1-9][0-9]*)(\.[0-9]+)? (no exponent, no leading + or -, no thousands separators); monetary amounts are non-negative. The number of fractional digits MUST NOT exceed the scale implied by the currency/asset (for example two for most ISO 4217 currencies, or assetScale when supplied). Arithmetic MUST be exact decimal arithmetic; comparisons against limits MUST use the full precision of the operands.

Trust establishment

Verification answers is this proof valid and does it satisfy the stated constraints? It does not answer should this issuer be believed? That decision rests on a deployment's trust policy, which is out of band but security-critical.

  • A relying party MUST evaluate the issuer of each Spending Authorization Credential against an explicit, configured set of trusted principals before honoring it. A payee-advertised list of acceptable credential issuers is an assertion by the payee and MUST NOT be treated as a trust root.
  • A payer agent or wallet that relies on a Merchant Credential MUST evaluate the trust anchor that issued it (for example an industry registry) against configured policy.
  • Trust policies SHOULD be scoped (issuer X may authorize spending only in currency Y up to amount Z) rather than all-or-nothing, and SHOULD support short validity and revocation so compromise is contained.
  • Where DID methods derive authority from external infrastructure (did:web from DNS/TLS, on-ledger methods from a ledger), deployers MUST account for that infrastructure in their threat model ().

Trust vocabulary

How a deployment decides which issuers to believe is local policy and is intentionally not standardized here. This specification does, however, define a small vocabulary so that a relying party's trust configuration can be expressed, exchanged, and audited consistently. A TrustAnchor is an entity, identified by a DID, whose attestations a deployment elects to rely on — for example the issuer of Spending Authorization Credential instances or of Merchant Credential instances. A relying party's configuration is a set of TrustedIssuer entries.

A TrustedIssuer entry has:

  • issuer — DID of the trusted TrustAnchor, REQUIRED.
  • issuerScope — an IssuerScope bounding what the issuer is trusted to authorize, RECOMMENDED; its absence means the deployment trusts the issuer without an additional scope bound.
  • validFrom / validUntil — optional validity window for the trust grant.

An IssuerScope MAY constrain:

  • currency — the only currency the issuer may authorize.
  • maxPerTransaction — ceiling the issuer may grant per transaction.
  • maxDailyTotal — ceiling the issuer may grant per day.
  • allowedCategories — service-category IRIs the issuer may authorize.

When honoring a credential, a relying party MUST confirm the credential's issuer matches a configured TrustedIssuer; when an IssuerScope is present it MUST reject (with credentialRejected) any credential whose constraints exceed that scope. Where both an IssuerScope and a credential constrain the same dimension, the more restrictive value applies, consistent with . Trust grants SHOULD be scoped and short-lived so that compromise is contained. A deployment MAY publish its trusted-issuer set as a signed list; the distribution format and any trust-registry resolution protocol are out of scope for this specification.

Verification

A conformant verifier presented with a credential defined by this specification MUST perform the following checks before relying on it. These are normative; the reference implementation (spec/verify.py) exercises them.

  • Proof. The proof MUST verify under ecdsa-jcs-2022, and its verificationMethod MUST be controlled by the credential's issuer.
  • Validity window. The current time MUST fall within [validFrom, validUntil], and validFrom MUST precede validUntil.
  • Revocation. A verifier MUST resolve the credentialStatus [[[BITSTRING-STATUS-LIST]]] entry and reject a revoked credential. (Status-list resolution is performed out of band and is not exercised by the offline reference harness.)
  • Trust. For a SpendingAuthorizationCredential, the issuer MUST be on the verifier's trusted-issuer list, and the more restrictive of the credential's limits and the issuer's issuerScope MUST apply.
  • Well-formed limits. Where present, maxPerTransaction MUST NOT exceed dailyLimit.

Security considerations

Delegated Spending Authority moves high-value decisions (spending) across trust boundaries. Implementations MUST protect private signing keys, SHOULD isolate agent keys in hardware or a separate process, and MUST evaluate issuer trust () before honoring credentials.

The table summarizes the principal threats and the mechanisms that address them.

ThreatMechanism
Credential theft / replay (use a stolen VC)Verification-method binding to the subject DID (key binding); the thief cannot produce the payer's proof.
Over-scoping beyond delegated authoritymaxPerTransaction, dailyLimit with its day boundary (daily-limit aggregation), and the more restrictive of credential and policy rule ().
Untrusted or over-scoped issuerExplicit, scoped trust policy and revocation via [[BITSTRING-STATUS-LIST]] ().
Unverified term injection via JSON-LD@protected context with no @vocab; unknown terms are dropped ().
DID-method infrastructure compromise (DNS/TLS, ledger)Out of scope for value transfer; deployers account for it in their trust policy (), and proofs are verified against DID state at proof.created (DID state at proof time).

Privacy considerations

Agents SHOULD disclose the minimum verifiable claims necessary for a transaction. Selective disclosure is not a property of the base [[vc-data-model-2.0]]; deployments that need it SHOULD use a mechanism that provides it, such as a Data Integrity cryptosuite with selective disclosure. Pairwise identifiers are compatible with these mechanisms.

Correlation can arise from DID resolution, repeated payer DIDs, issuer identifiers, and settlement metadata. High-privacy deployments SHOULD analyze timing and identifier rotation, and SHOULD NOT place principal or end-user PII in a Spending Authorization Credential, which travels to relying parties and potentially payees.

Internationalization considerations

The currency member SHOULD carry an ISO 4217 alphabetic code for fiat currencies. A deployment MAY use a non-ISO asset code (for example a token symbol); when it does, both parties MUST agree on the code's meaning and scale out of band, and an assetScale SHOULD be supplied. Decimal amounts use a . as the decimal separator regardless of locale (see ); locale-specific formatting is a presentation concern and MUST NOT appear in the wire format. Human-readable strings such as merchantName MAY use VC 2.0 language-tagged values.

Accessibility considerations

Delegated Spending Authority defines machine-to-machine data objects and a verification algorithm; it has no user interface and therefore introduces no direct accessibility requirements. User agents that surface DSA information to people — for example a wallet prompting for approval under requiresApprovalAbove — are expected to follow general Web accessibility guidance ([[WCAG22]]) for that presentation, and any human-readable strings carried in messages should use VC 2.0 language-tagged values () so assistive technologies can present them correctly.

Conformance classes

This specification defines the following conformance classes:

DSA credential issuer / principal
Software, controlled by a principal, that produces conforming Spending Authorization Credential instances (and optionally Merchant Credential and Payment Capability Credential instances). It MUST issue credentials secured per whose credentialSubject.id is the payer agent DID, and SHOULD carry a credentialStatus entry ([[BITSTRING-STATUS-LIST]]).
DSA credential verifier
Software that, given a Spending Authorization Credential (typically within a verifiable presentation), re-verifies the credential proof, the verification-method binding to the subject DID (key binding), the validity window, and revocation/suspension status when present, and enforces the credential's constraints. It MUST resolve DID state as of proof.created (DID state at proof time) and MUST treat absent credential constraints as not granting authority.
DSA trust evaluator
Software that evaluates a credential's issuer against an explicit, configured trust policy () before the credential is honored. It MUST confirm the issuer matches a configured TrustedIssuer, MUST reject (with credentialRejected) any credential whose constraints exceed a present IssuerScope, and MUST NOT treat a payee-advertised list of acceptable issuers as a trust root.

Security and Privacy Self-Review

This appendix answers the questions of the W3C TAG Self-Review Questionnaire: Security and Privacy [[SECURITY-PRIVACY-QUESTIONNAIRE]]. Delegated Spending Authority defines data objects and a verification algorithm rather than a browser feature, so several questions are answered as not applicable.

What information does this feature expose, and for what purposes?
Payer/payee/issuer DIDs and the constraints in a Spending Authorization Credential. The purpose is to let a relying party authorize spending and let a verifier audit it. Objects expose only what is needed to evaluate the delegated authority; see the next answer.
Do features expose the minimum information necessary?
Agents are directed to disclose the minimum verifiable claims (). The base [[vc-data-model-2.0]] lacks selective disclosure, so deployments needing it are pointed to a selective-disclosure cryptosuite. PII SHOULD NOT be placed in credentials that travel to relying parties and payees.
Do features handle personally identifiable or financial information?
Yes — spending authority. Limits and category constraints bound exposure. Correlation risks (repeated DIDs, issuer identifiers) are addressed in .
Does this feature introduce new state that persists across sessions?
Relying parties retain a configured trust policy and may retain credentials for audit. The specification directs that private keys and full credential payloads not be retained beyond reconciliation needs.
How does the feature deal with information from origins a user is not interacting with / third parties?
Trust is never inherited from the message sender: a relying party MUST evaluate credential issuers against its own configured trust roots and MUST NOT treat a payee's advertised list of acceptable issuers as a trust root ().
What are the feature's safe defaults, and what happens on error?
Undefined JSON-LD terms are not given protocol semantics (@protected, no @vocab), processors MUST NOT make authorization decisions from them, unrecognized cryptosuites are rejected, and absent credential constraints are treated as not permitted by the credential rather than permissive.
What are the risks of abuse, and the mitigations?
The threat/mitigation table in enumerates credential theft/replay, over-scoping beyond delegated authority, untrusted issuers, and term injection, together with the mechanisms that address each.
Does the feature expose sensors, the underlying platform, native UI, or allow access to other devices?
Not applicable; Delegated Spending Authority is a transport-agnostic data model.
How does the feature behave in private-browsing/incognito modes?
Not applicable as a browser feature. Agents that need unlinkability are directed to pairwise/rotated identifiers ().
