The trust layer for AI-agent payments

AVP-Micro is a vendor-neutral way to grant, prove, and verify delegated spending authority for autonomous agents — built on W3C Verifiable Credentials, DIDs, and Data Integrity proofs, and designed to sit above any settlement rail.

The problem

Agents spend money. Who says they can?

Autonomous agents pay for marginal resources — tokens, rows, minutes, inference calls — at a frequency and granularity humans never needed. Every serious deployment has to answer three questions, verifiably, without handing each agent an unlimited corporate card.

01

Who is paying?

Not just "which API key" — an identity recognizable across services and audits, bound to cryptographic keys. AVP-Micro identifies every party by DID.

02

Under what authority?

The agent should spend only within rules a principal set: per-transaction caps, daily limits, allowed counterparties, categories, time windows, and revocation.

03

Did value move — and what was delivered?

Settlement is distinct from authorization. There must be a clear, signed link between agreed terms, execution, and delivery — for reconciliation and disputes.

✕
Cards & bank rails were built for human consent and chargeback regimes, with fees that punish true micropayments.
✕
OAuth & API keys express access to an API, not spending policy under a principal's mandate — and rarely port across merchants.
✕
Platform wallets solve one ecosystem but reinforce lock-in: each marketplace becomes its own root of trust.
The architectural insight

Separate trust from settlement

Make the split explicit, and the same authorization semantics survive when the rail changes — and many rails can coexist behind one wallet policy engine.

Layer 1 Trust & authorization — AVP-Micro
Standardizes who may spend, under what terms, and how it's proven — independent of how value actually moves.
Portable identity

How it works

Check authority once, pay many times

A principal issues one bounded credential. Each charge is then a lightweight, request-bound signed message. Every object below is a real, signed test vector from this repository.

STEP 2AuthorizeAgent signs approval + presents credential
STEP 3ExecuteWallet enforces policy & records the charge
STEP 4ReceiptPayee signs confirmation of delivery

The challenges

What makes this hard — and how the suite answers it

Machine-native micropayments run into economic, technical, trust, and operational walls. Each maps to a concrete mechanism in the bundles.

Economic

Unit economics & streaming

Per-transaction fees dominate when the payment is tiny.

→ Signed accruals & session budgets, a pricing-model vocabulary, and rail-pluggable settlement so low-fee rails drop in without changing authorization.
Technical

Replay & request binding

Old approvals must not be replayed against new charges.

→ Every payment binds a requestHash and quoteDigest, with nonces and short expiry; the simulator enforces single-use consumption at runtime.
Trust & interop

Fragmented identity & opaque delegation

Without portable identity, every platform is its own trust root.

→ Parties are identified by DID; authority is an issuer-signed VC with explicit, machine-verifiable policy — recognizable across independent merchants.
Operational

Revocation, audit & disputes

Rights change; enterprises need who-authorized-what evidence.

→ BitstringStatusList revocation, an append-only signed chain from authorization to receipt, and a full refund / reversal / dispute lifecycle.

How it compares

Where AVP-Micro sits among agent-payment protocols

Most agent-commerce protocols orchestrate checkout or move money. AVP-Micro's job is the portable, merchant-verifiable spending mandate — and it bridges to, rather than fights, the front-runners.

ProtocolPrimary layerAuthorization modelRelationship to AVP-Micro
AVP-Micro Trust + authorization W3C VC mandate (SpendingAuthorizationCredential), ecdsa-jcs-2022, settlement-agnostic —
AP2 (Google, 60+ partners) Trust + authorization W3C VC mandates (Intent / Cart / Payment) Closest analog; interop target via the SD-JWT-VC bridge
Skyfire KYA / KYAPay Identity + authz + rail Signed KYA JWT, OAuth2/OIDC Direct competitor at the mandate layer; candidate bridge target
ACP (OpenAI + Stripe) Commerce + payment Stripe Shared Payment Token Competes on intent authorization; token, not a VC
UCP (Google + Shopify) Commerce orchestration Delegates to AP2 Composes onto the mandate layer; not a rival
Mastercard Agent Pay Card-rail impl. Agentic Tokens Commercial competitor at the rails layer
Visa Intelligent Commerce / TAP Card-rail impl. Network-specific Commercial competitor at the rails layer
x402 / L402 / MPP Settlement (HTTP 402) Rail-specific challenge Complementary; bridge / settlement target

✓ Largely addressed by the state of the art

  • Moving money: Open Payments, Lightning, stablecoin flows, MPP-style HTTP 402
  • Request integrity & replay: HTTP Message Signatures (RFC 9421), DPoP (RFC 9449)
  • General-purpose portable identity: W3C DID & VC standards
  • Internally-coherent mandate stacks: SD-JWT-VC (Verifiable Intent / AP2)

△ Still underspecified for cross-vendor agent commerce

  • Policy that survives a rail change (caps, allowlists, session budgets) as normative artifacts
  • Binding quotes, sessions, and accruals into one auditable story
  • A normative bridge so one agent's authority verifies on either stack
  • A cross-vendor, cryptographically-bound refund / reversal / chargeback flow
Conformance

Guarantees demonstrated, not just asserted

A shared Python harness generates every signed test vector and validates all six bundles, and a protocol simulator enforces the runtime behaviour static vectors can't.

Signed test vectors

Every normative requirement is exercised by deterministic, signed vectors generated by generate.py — across all six bundles.

Offline validation

validate.py checks Turtle, JSON-LD expansion, JSON Schema, and SHACL — fully offline, with vendored W3C contexts.

Protocol simulator

Runs the full signed-message flow with real proofs and wallet-side policy against a play-money ledger — caps, replay, expiry, budgets, dispute lifecycle.
